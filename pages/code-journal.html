  • See The Big Picture

    November 14, 2018

    I guess this is an important reason why I can made through most of my non-whiteboarding technical interviews: I have a big picture of the tech field in my mind. It may not be complete or accurate, but I do have my own opinions on a variety of technologies. So I am not afraid of answering any technical questions, except for whiteboarding. üòÖ How do I get that picture? Well, whenever I go to an event and talk to people about a new topic, I search it in this big picture, and try to make it relevant some way. It may fit in one particular area, or it can be just a general concept. If I do find a fit, I will try to ask questions based on my understanding of that area. This will make it easier to have a two way conversation. If I don‚Äôt find it, I still want to learn, then I show my interests and ask questions along the way. Either way, I will learn new things, and add it to my picture. And next time when I come across that topic again, I can claim that I know a little bit about it. If I am very interested in one particular area, I will learn more from other resources, like online courses or books. But I do need to know the keywords for the search. So basically I am building my vocabulary from each conversation, then expand my knowledge from self learning. Sometimes I do meet people who are great teachers, in that case I just learn a lot from those people.

    But there are so many technologies out there, how can I learn all of them? Well, in my opinion, this is actually the beauty of the tech world. No one can be an expert of everything. Even a principal engineer still doesn‚Äôt know something. So if you see it from another angle, that means you can also teach other people, even when you are just a student/intern. You are the expert of your projects at least. As you gain more experiences, you might be an expert of the tools you use. But you do need to have a broader knowledge on technologies. That way, when you try to explain people your work, you can make the topic relevant by using some terms your audience may know.

    My personal advice is: don‚Äôt be intimidated by other people‚Äôs n+ years experiences. That means they might be an expert in some particular fields, and they might have more hands-on experiences than you generally. But only you know your projects the best, and you should be the best salesman of your work. As long as you can explain what you did, how you made it work, and why you made the key decisions, you are good. Keep in mind the fact that there always something people don‚Äôt know. Being curious and keeping learning is the best way to accumulate your knowledge. This habit will help you in long term. To me, it helps me build confidence, so I am not scared by any non-whiteboarding interviews now. It also helps me identify my passion, and what I am looking for in my next position. All these things can change in the future, especially after I achieve my short-term learning goals. I will keep putting new things into that picture, and identify my interests periodically. Maybe one day, once I think I am ready, I may just start my own tech business. Who knows! üòâ

    Last thing I want to mention to my C9 friends: look at what you have learned from class session, capstone and internship. Try to put them on your own picture, and connect the dots. You will be amazed by how much you have learned and achieved in the past 9.5 months. You should really be proud of yourself. üëç

  • How To Build Your Confidence & Figure Out What You Want

    - To C9 Adies -

    November 6, 2018

    Although I am having several interviews per week right now, I don't feel nervous at all, even for the important ones. Why? Because I had this long conversation with a super psychologist/hacker at Chef three weeks ago. He made me believe that I should only apply for the companies I share the same value with, and I should choose what I want to do in my next job. He believed that I shouldn't worry about getting a job at all, although it's the first time we met. But he did have something to support his point: 1) I am an Adie. He thought the Adie intern at Chef is a full-time employer because she is very professional. Also he heard good things about Ada before. 2) After talking to me about various topics for more than 2 hours, he believed I am totally qualified as a Junior developer. 3) The market is great.

    This conversation itself won't make me 100% confident in myself, but it inspired me a lot. I no longer felt overwhelmed about job search. I made a plan based on his advice. I filtered out the companies I wasn't ready to apply or I thought would be a bad fit. I was very clear about what type of positions I should not apply at that moment. So I only applied for certain types of positions (Site Reliability Engineer, Full-Stack Engineer, and Backend Engineer) from a certain group of companies (small & mid-size). There was one execption, but only one. And I gave myself two weeks to test this stategy out. If I didn't get anything after two weeks, I would adjust my strategy accordingly, then test it again. And three weeks later, I got 4 onsites and some other ongoing interviews sheduled on my calendar. Actually I haven't failed any interviews yet, because I make sure I am passionate about almost all of the positions I am applying. And as I mentioned at the beginning, I don't feel nervous about interviews now. They are just normal conversations between normal people. I am not looking up at anyone. The team is trying to find a good candidate to be part of it, and I am looking for a team I want to be part of. It's that simple.

    But you still need to show people your capability, right? Code challenge is one way to measure it (definitely the most popular way in the tech field right now), but I would say there are many other options. And this is the best part about Ada. Our instructors worked really hard to put a variety of techniques used in the industry into our curriculum. If you follow through and do a little bit extra work, you will come up with a portfolio like what I have on this website. People can definitely tell your growth from the list of projects: CLI program in Feb, a complicated ecommerce site in April, then a functional mobile app in July. Why wouldn't they want to hire you? I actually used as many free resources as I can to make it even better. GitHub Pages and Heroku are free, so I hosted 3 of my React projects and this website on GitHub Pages, as well as all live projects with UI on Heroku. One of my interviewers actually told me he played Litter Patrol for a while. It was one of the simplest React app we did at Ada, and I only had 3 commits there. But it doesn't matter. It's my work, and it shows how I learn new things. Another tool I am using is LinkedIn. I built my profile there, I set my status as looking for a job, I linked almost all of my tech accounts there, and I am actively advocating for things I believe. If some recruiters like my work, they can just contact me directly. Coding questions are something we cannot avoid at this moment, but we can build our confidence based on our real work. And there are plenty of great companies will take this into account.

    What if I don't feel good showing all my projects or GitHub account to my potential employers? Well, if you have some time, I would suggest you polish some of your favorite ones (maybe host them somewhere), and put the link on your resume at least. It doesn't have to be perfect. You can talk about all the overnight works, how you tackled the problems, and what you learned from it. We were students, and we were supposed to make mistakes during the learning process. The more you reflect on what you have learned, the better you understand the material, and yourself. The better you know yourself, your strength and passion, the easier for you to figure out what you want. Then you get into this positive cycle: you know what you want, you choose what you should spend time on, you focus your limited energy on those things, you get some positive feedback, you are confident about yourself, then you get what you want. When you are ready to move on to the next level, you do it again, and you will get there. All of these require hard work and some strategy, but it's better than being lost and getting into a bad cycle, which probably makes you feel exhausted and doubt your capabilities. If you made it through Ada, it means a lot! So trust yourself, you will succeed as our instructors said. You just need to figure out your own way to get there. It's a real problem you need to tackle, but you know how to do it, because you're an Adie. üíó

  • Dissecting Tech Interviews

    November 1, 2018

    Today's topic is about what you can expect after getting an invitation to officially start your interview process with a company for a software engineering position. The process really depends on which company you are interviewing with, but usually it involves a couple of standard components, including 1) a screening interview with a technical recruiter over the phone, 2) a take-home challenge in a particular form, 3) a screening interview with a decision maker over the phone or onsite, and 4) an onsite interview with a couple of future coworkers involved. So far, I have seen combinations of 1-2-3-4, 1-3-2-4, 1-2-4, and 2-3-(1)-4. And don't be surprised if you have another type of interview not listed here. Just be open-minded and get prepared the best you can. Also there might another person involved in this process, the interview coordinator. This person can be the technical recruiter you talk to, can also be another person who just takes care of all interviews for that position. You should probably contact this person regarding interview progress. Now let's go to details about each one.

    ~~ Talking to A Technical Recruiter ~~

    This is definitely a two-way converstation. The recruiter probably will ask you about how much you know about the company and why you apply. Don't worry if you don't really understand their business. Do a brief research, and be honest about what you know. But you definitely should show your passion. If you feel you need fake on that, rethink about if you should apply. You should only apply for the companies that you have at least a neutral impression. I won't sugguest you waste your time on companies you don't want to work for. This is also the best time to ask any questions about the company's products and finicial status, especially for small and mid-size companies. Also the structure of their organization. The technical recruiter may not be able to answer technical questions, but they should be quite familiar with the big picture of the company, like how well the company is doing and their plan to expand the team. I just covered a couple of topics I usually ask. You should come up with your own list before the conversation.

    In term of your background, you should be comfortable to explain your projects to a non-engineering person, and why you think you are a good fit for this position. Also, visa sponsorship is something foreign candidates may need, so the recruiter probably will ask about this for a doublecheck.

    I usually spend about 0.5 - 1 hour to prepare for this interview. I will learn a little bit about the company. I may download and try their products if it's a mobile app. Then I will compare my resume with the job description, and make sure to highlight my relevant skills, and prepare questions based on this whole process.

    The recruiter may wrap up the conversation with some information about the next step. If this is not provided, you probably should ask to get some idea about their timeline. Usually the whole hiring process takes about 3 - 4 weeks. It makes sense if you think about the four steps I mentioned early. But this is also company-specific. Since I am targeting at small and mid-size companies right now, my experiences is more representative of this group.

    ~~ Take-home Challenge ~~

    So far I have encountered three types of challenge questions: 1) timed online problem, 2) timed offline problem, and 3) untimed offline design question. I would suggest different strategies for each one of them. But general rules would apply to all of them, like having a good editor with debugging capability ready especially for 1 & 2, solving problems step by step, and saving your work gradually. Now let's look into them in more details.

    1. Timed Online Problem

    A popular platform for this type of problem is HackerRank. You are supposed to solve one or more problems within a time limit, usually a couple of hours. There will be description of the problem, some sample inputs and outputs, requirements, and tests. You might be able to download the samples to your local machine. There is be an editor where you choose your perferred language and input your code. If you haven't tried any of these platforms before, I highly recommend you try the sample test on HankerRank to get familiar with the UI, therefore you don't waste anytime on getting familiar with the environment when you do your real test. This can also prevent your from getting panic if you cannot figure out how it works during a real test.

    In terms of the answer, you usually know how many tests you pass after clicking Run Code button. You may need to pass all of the known tests (sample inputs & outputs) to get additional test being ran. For the additional ones, there may not be any clue why you fail, so you need to figure out what are the cases have not been covered by your current code. This can be frustrating. Try your best to debug, but don't worry if you cannot finish it. My personal experience indicates that passing all test cases is not the only way you get into next round. So just calm down, and do your best. Usually you can submit as many times as you want within the time limit. So I usually submit whenever I make some substantial progress, like passing most of the initial tests. I will submit again once I pass another one.

    Once you finish the problem, give yourself a break and don't think about the problem for now. But do remember to save your code in a file, so that if you make it through, you can revisit your answer, and prepare for any questions about improvement of your code in your next interview. Challenge questions are intimitating, but keep in mind that it may not be the only criteria during the hiring process. You just need to show your hard work and the way you tackle a problem. Don't worry about things you cannot control. Actually I have my personal philosophy about whiteboarding/coding challenges: if I am evaluated just by this type of exercise, I may not want to work at that company at all. I am a human, not a machine. You may not agree with me on this, and it's totally ok. If this is the only way you can get into your dream company, then working hard definitely increases your chance. No matter what, good luck!

    2. Timed Offline Problem

    For the problem I did, two links were provided in the email: problem link and submission link. I was tracked when I opened the problem link, that's when the timer started. So basically I was supposed to submit my solution to the other link within the time limit, 2 hours in this case.

    The problem can be just text. There won't be an IDE for you to write and test your code. That said, you will need to create your own file(s), create and use your own test cases, and write necessary instructions about how to run and test your code (because your perferred language might be new to your reviewer). I use Ruby for code challenges, so I actually wrote an instruction based on Ada's Jump Start Environment Setup. I also recommended repl.it, because it's actually very convenient to use for this situation. I wrote tests for all cases I could think of after the method, but I didn't have time to create a test file for that. I think it would be great to write automatic tests to show your TDD skills, but it might not be practical given the time constraint.

    My advice for this type of problem: read through the email, and get prepared for things you can do in advance, like writing a draft instruction for installations, and maybe creating a testing template if you want to have automatic tests in your solution. To make sure they do get my solution, I actually attached my solution again when I updated my status via email... I don't think it's necessary, but it's a 4.2KB file anyway üòÖ

    3. Untimed Offline Design Question

    I acutally don't know how often this type of questions are given for interviews. My personal impression is: the jobseeker must be quite familiar with the tools being used by the team, and the questions need to be solved with these tools. So not like general challenge questions, you may be limited to use one particular language or framework, and you probably should have implemented similar projects before. So your experiences probably match the position very well.

    In my case, I was asked to implement a couple of simple features for an app. Code base with vague outline codes and a couple of TO-DO stubs were provided. It's a quite open project, so there is no hard deadline and detailed requirements, although I was recommended to finished within a week. I basically tackled the problem like what I did at Ada. Created an ERD first, then filled out the TO-DOs by category, wrote test stubs as needed, implemented required functions, finally cleaned up the code. Since a funtional app was not required, I didn't really try to make it work. I did mannualy test a private helper method which was not covered by the test suite to make sure it actually works as intended. As usual, I used git for version control.

    I added notes in the email when I sent the answer back, and was told my work will be reviewed within a week. I am still waiting for the response, so not sure if my work is good enough to pass the test. I will update here once I get an answer for that. Figure crossed. üôè

    Update: I got a response regarding my onsite interview with the team. It seems my approach was good enough for this type of task. And it's ok to just write test stubs for this project. üòå

    ~~ Talking to Hiring Manager/Someone Important ~~

    Although you can get some insight about the position from the recruiter, this is the perfect time to learn about what the team does and uses, and if the position actually aligns well with your interest. You may talk to the hiring manager, or a senior engineer in that team, who is supposed to be able to answer most of your technical questions. They are also the person who will decide if you should be invited for an onsite interview conducted by a group of people, probably your future coworkers and collaborators.

    Usually this is another round of phone interview. But sometimes it can be done onsite if you are local and would like to visit their office. I would recommend to go in person if it's an option, because I see many benefits doing it. You can learn more about their culture and working environment. You will talk to a person face to face, which I think is easier for communication than a phone call. If you make it to the real onsite, you already know how to get there and won't get lost. üòÖ Anyway, I had very positive experience for one of my prior-onsite onsite interview.

    To prepare for this type of interview, make sure you can explain your projects very well. They are the people who can understand what you have done and raise questions about implementations, even when they are not quite familiar with the specific technologies you use. If you're still learning something, then be honest about that, and express your passion about learning more. Be confident and show you hard work as well. Based on my experiences, being curious and self-motivated are two important qualifications managers are looking for in software engineering candidates.

    In terms of your own questions, they can be anything you care about for your next job, and maybe some technical questions. I usually ask about terms I cannot understand on the job description. Sometimes I can find the answer online, but I like to listen to my interviewers' answer, not only because they usually give a better explanation on the topic given their technical background, I can also get an idea about their personality based on this conversation. Usually it's also a good reflection of the company's culture. I would say, this might be the best opportunity to learn about your future boss, who may not be your manager but definitely will have a great impact on your life if you get hired. So make sure you have a positive feeling about this interview when you make your final decision.

  • Job Search Strategies

    October 31, 2018

    Happy Halloween! üëª After two weeks of job search, I finally get a chance to share my experiences here. Actually everything is going so well. I have 6 active interviews at different stages, and 3 to start in the near future. Job market is pretty good. Today's journal will focus on resume, LinkedIn, referral, and schedule. I will share my interview experiences in the next journal.

    Resume üìù

    Last time when I searched for a job, I used MS Word to write resumes. Since job seekers are always recommended to customize their resume for each position, I still remember the large number of similar but different files I had back then. I have to say it wasn't easy to keep track of them.

    This time, I changed my strategy. First, I switched to Google Docs. I picked one of their resume template, so I didn't need to worry about styling. Then I had one base resume, which basically contained everything I need in a general purpose resume: Title (name, phone, email, and links), Summary, Experiences, Community Engagement, and Education. So whenever I want to update my resume, I change content is this base version. And when I am ready to apply for a new position, I will make a copy of this file, change the file name to include company's name, and add another session below Summary: Top Relevant Skills. This session will be a list of the experiences/skills relevant to that particular position, like a highlighter. I can download the file as pdf, or I can simply share my link with recruiters. The advantage of sharing a file is you can update your resume after submitting your application, then whoever look at the file will be able to see the updated version as well. Super convenient! So far, this new strategy worked pretty well.

    LinkedIn üë©üèª‚Äçüíª

    LinkedIn is very important. It's one of the best ways to find connections. Although some people may not feel comfortable to connect with "strangers", there are many people out there who would like to offer help. Also it's a great place to build your own profile, show your hard work, and express your opinions. I think I finally figure out how to make LinkedIn work for me. And I actually got one of my interviews through LinkedIn.

    Referral ü§ù

    Based on my experiences, application by referral is the best way to get into the door. I submitted a couple of applications online directly. I have only seen rejection email or no response so far. On the other hand, I got response for almost all of my applications with a referral. And for the ones with a response, I started the official interview process pretty soon, usually within a week. The fastest one was within one business day. I am glad I went to a great meetup during class session. I got most of the current interviews because of help from people in that group. ‚ù§Ô∏è

    Schedule üìÜ

    Since I am doing a full-time internship, I have very limited time for interviews. A good shedule strategy is another thing I need to think about. I chose to WFH on Monday and Friday to take phone interviews, then Tuesday through Thursday for onsite interviews when I am in the town. For phone interview, I am using Google voice. I usually have 2 - 3 interviews and probably 1 - 2 take-home challenges per week. I will do challenges during weekend, when I have a large block of time. So far, everything goes quite smoothly. I don't have much stress since I spread everything throughout the week, so I will just focus on one thing at a time.

  • CSS Grid vs. Flexbox

    Mar 17, 2018

    To me, CSS is like a huge black box. There are so many propertie and value pairs available hiding somewhere. So I was kinda frustrated ‚òπÔ∏è at some point and had a strong feeling üò§ again... It's not because CSS is hard. Compared to CS fundamentals and design problems, I know CSS is much easier to understand. It just confuses me, and I don't have the time to get everything clear.     Here is a brief summary: For Grid, justify is always along the row direction ‚û°Ô∏è, and align is always along the column direction ‚¨áÔ∏è. For Flexbox, justify is along the flow direction, and align is perpendicular to the flow direction.

    I am glad I asked for help again for this particular problem. It saved me a lot of time, so that I can make this site much better.üòÑ So the moral of this story is: NEVER GIVE UP!
